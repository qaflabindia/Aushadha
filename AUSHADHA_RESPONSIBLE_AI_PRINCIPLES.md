# Aushadha: Responsible AI Principles

Aushadha is a Clinical Intelligence platform that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) and Knowledge Graphs to extract, structure, and analyze patient health information. Given the critical nature of healthcare workflows, the integration of Artificial Intelligence must be governed by strict ethical, clinical, and privacy-centric guidelines.

This document outlines the core Responsible AI (RAI) principles that govern the design, development, and deployment of the Aushadha platform.

---

## 1. Clinical Augmentation, Not Replacement (Human-in-the-Loop)

**Principle:** AI should serve as a powerful cognitive assistant to healthcare professionals, not an autonomous oracle making definitive clinical diagnoses.

*   **Decision Support:** The knowledge graph and conversational AI tools (e.g., retrieving patient history, listing extracted symptoms or medications) are designed to surface relevant facts quickly so the physician can make informed decisions.
*   **Verification:** Every piece of information extracted by the LLM (Entities, Relationships, Chunks) must be traceable back to the original source text. The Aushadha UI allows users to click on an insight and view the exact paragraph from the clinical note from which it was derived. The AI does not generate unseen clinical facts; it structures what already exists.

## 2. Privacy, Security, and Data Sovereignty

**Principle:** Patient data (PHI) is sacred. AI models must operate under the assumption of absolute privacy, respecting multi-tenancy rules and data residency laws (e.g., HIPAA, GDPR, India DPDP Act).

*   **Logical Isolation:** As documented in the Security Architecture, all data ingested into the Neo4j Knowledge Graph is strictly isolated geographically and logically using the `owner_email` and `target_user_email` constructs. A patient or doctor can never accidentally query an unauthorized patient's data.
*   **Stateless Inference:** LLMs do not train on patient data. Aushadha utilizes Zero-Data Retention policies with cloud LLM providers, ensuring the prompts and completions are immediately discarded after inference.
*   **Local LLM Capability:** For the highest security tier, Aushadha supports running inferences entirely on-premise using local open-source models (e.g., Llama-3, MedAlpaca) via Ollama. This guarantees that PHI never leaves the local network or physical hardware.

## 3. Transparency and Explainability

**Principle:** Healthcare providers must understand *why* the AI generated a specific response or structured an entity in a certain way.

*   **Traceable Knowledge Graphs:** Unlike pure "black box" RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) applications, Aushadha builds an explicit Knowledge Graph. The intermediate reasoning steps—Entities, Relationships, and Document Chunks—are visualized and inspectable. 
*   **Source Citations:** Conversational answers generated by the chatbot must include citations referencing the unique `Chunk ID` or `Document Name` used to synthesize the response.
*   **Model Agnosticism:** The platform makes it transparent which model (e.g., Gemini, GPT-4, Llama-3) is being utilized for a given task, allowing clinical administrators to configure and evaluate the most reliable engine for their specific clinical domain.

## 4. Fairness, Bias Mitigation, and Non-Discrimination

**Principle:** The AI should provide consistent, high-quality analysis regardless of a patient's demographic background, gender, or localized language nuances.

*   **Ailment Agnostic Design:** The extraction pipelines are driven by generic, agnostic schemas (e.g., `GENERALIZED_EHR_SCHEMA.json`). The system avoids hardcoding heuristic biases toward specific clinical presentations unless explicitly guided by the configured clinical playbook.
*   **Language Inclusivity:** The platform integrates multi-lingual capabilities (e.g., Hindi, English) in the UI and leverages LLMs capable of cross-lingual reasoning to ensure that clinical notes written in regional dialects are parsed with the same efficacy as standard medical English.

## 5. Reliability, Safety, and Fallback Mechanisms

**Principle:** Clinical AI systems must default to safety in the presence of uncertainty or systemic failure.

*   **Hallucination Guardrails:** The system prompt architecture enforces strict instructional boundaries. LLMs are instructed to output "Unknown" or explicitly state a lack of information rather than synthesizing clinically plausible but factually incorrect data (hallucinations).
*   **Schema Enforcement:** All extracted data must conform to a rigorous JSON schema before being committed to the database. If an LLM outputs malformed or nonsensical data, the system triggers automated retry loops or gracefully rejects the commit.
*   **Graceful Degradation:** If external LLM APIs experience outages, the system architecture natively supports falling back to local models or downgrading to standard keyword-based search functionality without crashing the clinical interface.

## 6. Accountability and Continuous Auditing

**Principle:** There must be a clear chain of accountability for AI-assisted clinical workflows, with ongoing monitoring of system performance.

*   **Audit Trails:** Every action, from document ingestion to schema updates and LLM inferences, is logged. The system tracks metrics such as processing time, token usage, and error rates.
*   **Feedback Loops:** Mechanisms for clinical staff to report inaccurate extractions or poor conversational responses, ensuring the continuous refinement of the underlying prompts, system templates, and extraction schemas.
